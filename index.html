Bots: a cultural tour

Martin O'Leary | @mewo2

[These are the slides from a talk I gave to the Digital Cultures Group in Manchester in May 2017. The audience were a group of "arts organisations and cultural producers"]

Who's this guy?

Before we start, I thought I'd say a little about who I am, so you can get a sense of where I'm coming from with this.

I'm a curator

Professionally, I'm the curator of Oriel Science, which is a small science exhibition centre, attached to Swansea University. I spend most of my time making and sourcing exhibits, and acting as a go-between between scientists and the public.

I'm a scientist

I'm also a working scientist - specifically I'm a glaciologist. I work on the flow and fracture of glacial ice, in Greenland and Antarctica.
I f you've seen anything in the news recently about a large iceberg about to break off from Antarctica, that's the work of our group. Watching the iceberg, that is. We didn't break it off.

I'm a "creative coder"

I'm also a "creative coder", which is to say that I enjoy making computers do things for the purposes of art and entertainment. Mostly these days, that means making bots.

What's a bot?

A bot is a machine that takes the place of a human in a communications medium

I trained as a mathematician, so I like to start from definitions. This is the definition I find most useful. As humans, we create systems for interacting with each other. If we take a machine and insert it into that system, playing the role of a human, then that's what I call a bot. The machine still obeys the rules of the system in the same way that a human would - we don't interact with the machine any differently to a human.
Here's an example of a bot. We created the telephone network as a way for humans to talk to each other. At some point, someone decided to plug a machine into that system. You call a number, expecting to talk to a human, but instead you talk to a machine. The affordances of the telephone haven't changed - the phone hasn't suddenly sprouted a keyboard and mouse. You still have to speak to the machine as you would to a human. From the perspective of the telephone network, you and the answering machine are the same kind of thing.

A bot is a machine computer program that takes the place of a human in a communications medium social network

Now, it's 2017, so most of the "machines" that we interact with are actually computer programs, and most of the communications media are actually social networks. But the underlying principles are the same. We're still looking at machines which interact on human terms. Some people like to argue that this is a dehumanising development. We're taking human communication, which has already been abstracted into 140 characters of text or whatever, and we're introducing machines into the mix, so we don't know if we're talking to a real human or not. I'm sympathetic to that view, but I would counter that there's a rehumanising aspect as well - we're forcing the machines to interact on our terms, not theirs. Also, probably worth saying that Twitter is by far the dominant social network for bots. There are various reasons for that, which I'm happy to talk about afterwards if anyone's interested.
So here are some examples of bots. This young lady who followed me on Twitter the other day is a classic spam bot. She follows people, posts some saucy photos, and tries to get you to click on this link, which turns out to be a malware delivery system.
This is a more topical use of bots. It's interesting to see how crude this stuff is - identical posts with no variation - very easy to filter out and block. Spam bots have been using much more sophisticated techniques for decades.
And this is what the tech industry would like you to think bots are all about. A sort of automated personal shopper. I can't say that I see the point, personally, but there's a ridiculous amount of money being spent on this, so basic economic theory says that either there's a point somewhere, or that the entire tech industry is a sham.

"Art bots"

Programs which generate "content"

Usually run on a schedule

Mostly disconnected posts

Often not interactive

...very like a spam bot

What I want to talk about today are "art bots" - bots made for artistic or entertainment purposes. They're sort of embarassingly similar to spam bots, but they're not usually trying to get you to click on a dodgy link.

Bot-ston (Martin O'Leary)

By way of example, this is one of my bots, Bot-ston. It generates new lyrics to the "Gaston" song from Disney's Beauty and the Beast, and sets them to music. You follow this on Twitter, and four times a day you get a little video clip that makes you smile.

Bots as performers

For the rest of the talk, I'm going to go through some bots, and talk about how they work aesthetically.

Performance

→ →
I want to start by talking about performance. If we think about a play, then we have a script, which is a static artifact, and then it gets performed, and at the end there might be a recording, which is also a static artifact. But neither of these things is the play. The play - the primary work of art - is the ephemeral thing in the middle, the performance. We can think the same way about computer programs. We have some source code, which we can save and archive, and we have some output, which we can also save and archive. But there's a thing in the middle, which we can't save, because it's a process, and it can't be stripped from its context. There's obviously a difference between a play and a computer program, because the performance of a play involves human beings, and the running of a computer program is an entirely inanimate thing. The difference with a bot though, is that the program is presenting itself as a human, or human-equivalent thing. So from the perspective of the audience, there is a performance happening, even if that's not really what's going on at the performer end.

CRASH__N__BURN (Stephanie Hyland)

Here's a simple example of a bot performance. CRASH__N__BURN does a reading of the script to the 199? movie Hackers, at fairly irregular intervals - I think whenever Stephanie really feels like it. It takes about three or four weeks to do a full run-through, then it goes quiet until the next performance.

Bots as performers

Long, slow, casual performance

Audience interaction

Bounded or unbounded

So, summing up some qualities of bots as performers. These are long performances that you wander in and out of. You don't pay attention to the whole thing. They're interactive - even if the bot doesn't actually reply, the audience interacts with each other. Part of what drives this is having the bot be on the same level as the audience - there's no privileged "stage" position. And finally, they can be bounded or unbounded, but finiteness definitely adds to the sense of performance.

Bots as magicians

I want to focus now on some similarities between bots and a particular kind of performer, which is magicians. [Full disclosure: I wrote this bit of the talk while watching The Prestige, it may not make any sense]

The Sorting Hat Bot (Darius Kazemi)

This is Darius Kazemi's "Sorting Hat Bot", which is a pretty straightforward fun thing. You follow it, and it sorts you into a Harry Potter house, and it writes a little poem about you.

To an experienced coder, this is not a complex thing. You get some word lists, and you pick from them at random. The rhyming bit seems hard at first, but actually you just get a rhyming dictionary.

@SortingBot how does this work lol?

— Ashlee Bailey-Seal (@AnnabelleLee94) April 6, 2015

@SortingBot how the fuck do u work

— Alizée 🥀 (@jcfuckash) July 8, 2016
To a large proportion of people, this is wizardry.
Darius wrote a thorough explanation of how the thing works, but the crucial bit that people care about is this one line at the end. The actual sorting is done completely at random. But there's no way to know that from following the bot.
The only secret of magic is that I'm willing to work harder on it than you think it's worth.

Penn Jillette

This makes me think of this quote from the magician Penn Jillette. The way magic works is by convincing you that the real explanation is less plausible than magic. So if you go to enough effort, people will assume you didn't, and that there's some simple trick instead. But people are terrible at judging the amount of work that computers do. Looking through every adjective in English, looking for one that rhymes with "born", is something a computer can do in microseconds. So it's very easy to convince people that you're doing something more complex than you are.

olivia taters (Rob Dubbin)

This is olivia taters, who's a sort of fake teenage girl. She assembles these sentences which are mostly nonsensical, but every so often there's something there which seems too real. So people assume there's something sophisticated going on. But there really isn't. Everything olivia says is assembled by splicing together fragments of real tweets.

Uncharted Atlas (Martin O'Leary)

This is one of mine, to show the flipside. You can deploy a simple procedure to appear complex, or you can do something really complex with a simple effect. This is generating fantasy maps, like you'd find in a cheap paperback sword and sorcery novel. The amount of computation that goes into these is obscene. They're doing erosion modelling, and routing rivers and all these things, for an output which is meant to look like a quick sketch.
By the way, after this had been running for about six months, I wrote a very lengthy piece explaining how it works.
The effect of this on the popularity of the bot was incredible. So that's a way in which bots are unlike magic - people do actually want to know how it works, and it doesn't seem to diminish their appreciation.

Bots as archivists

I want to talk a little bit about bots as record-keepers, journalists and archivists.

Parliament WikiEdits (Ed Summers)

This is a pretty simple example. It watches Wikipedia for edits made by IP addresses assigned to parliament buildings, and posts links to those edits. You can see where your elected representatives are trying to shape your information sources. This is a sort of journalistic project, but not something a human would ever be bothered to do - certainly not as an ongoing project, and certainly not in real time.

Editing The Gray Lady (@j_e_d)

This is the same thing, almost in reverse. Here we're watching for changes in the New York Times website. We can see how headlines are edited as stories develop. This is the sort of thing that doesn't make it into conventional archives, which would only store the "final form" of each story.

Anders Hoff aka inconvergent

On a more artistic note, this is the website of a Norwegian artist called Anders Hoff. He makes what he calls "generative art", pieces driven by code, by random algorithms which create 2d and 3d forms. There's a sort of tension here between the final image as an artwork, and the code as a description of all possible artworks.

generative bot (Anders Hoff)

Something he's done which I love is that he's set up a bot which regularly runs each of his algorithms and generates a new piece, tagged with the version number of the code, and a random seed. So you build up a kind of sketchbook, showing the evolution of the ideas in time, but also an exploration of all the possibilities of the random algorithm.

Bots as curators

Finally, I want to talk a bit about how bots work as curators of collections.

Museum Bot (Darius Kazemi)

This is one of the most common kinds of bots - it has a large pool of images, in this case the catalogue of the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, and it chooses from them at random and posts a little bit about that piece. This is nice, and it's an effective way of sharing content, but it's a bit vague and undirected for my tastes.

Old School Flyers (Darius Kazemi)

What I think are really great, are hyper-specific bots, like this one which shares old school hip-hop flyers. This is a much more curated experience, and it gives the audience more scope to control what they want to see. If I follow Museum Bot, I get everything from the Met, even if I'm only interested in pottery, say. This is focused delivery of the stuff I want to see.

Moon Shot Bot (Darius Kazemi)

This works really well for collections which would be a bit intimidating to look at all at once. The photos from the Apollo program are amazing, but to be honest I've had enough after about half a dozen. By drip-feeding them over time, you get a much better experience. It sort of worms its way into your life. And the archive is still there if you want a more full-on experience.

NYPL Dogs (Ashley Blewer)

Of course, if you have a big and varied archive, you can easily slice and dice it to make something more focused. This shows all the dogs in the New York Public Library digital collection.

Medieval Death Bot (Soren Häxan)

Images are popular, but you can also have text-based archives. This is sharing deaths from medieval coroner's reports. Some of them are darkly comic, but over time they build to give a real sense of medieval life.

Novice Art Blogger (Matthew Plummer-Fernandez)

We can also use collections as part of a larger project. This is taking images from the collection of the Tate, and putting it through some image recognition code, then attempting to generate "art criticism".

Archillect (Murat Pak)

Everything I've shown you so far has been using a random selection from a human-curated selection. It's curation, but maybe not super interesting curation. This is an attempt at automating curation. It posts images, looks at how many likes and retweets they get, and tries to find more images which are similar to the popular ones. Over time it has evolved a particular aesthetic, which I always think feels a bit Ridley Scott. I should add that some people take issue with this bot because it doesn't provide attribution for its images - the author claims this is because that's technically difficult, which I don't think is a very convincing argument for unethical behaviour.
